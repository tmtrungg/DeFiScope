1. The whole story in one picture

DeFiScope detects price-manipulation attacks: transactions where an attacker deliberately distorts a token's price inside a DeFi protocol and profits from the distortion. The paper's chain of reasoning, which the rest of this document unpacks piece by piece:

The single most important design fact
The LLM never decides "this is an attack." It answers one narrow question per step of a transaction: "did this token's price go up or down?" Everything else — reconstructing what the transaction did, matching attack patterns, the final verdict — is ordinary, deterministic Python. Think of a smoke detector: the LLM is the smoke sensor, the hand-written rules are the alarm circuit. When you evaluate this baseline, you are evaluating the sensor inside that fixed circuit.

2. DeFi from zero — the six ideas you need

2.1 Blockchain, accounts, and "user invocations" §II

A blockchain is a shared ledger. Two kinds of accounts live on it: EOAs (externally-owned accounts — a person with a private key) and contract accounts (CAs — programs called smart contracts, whose code executes automatically). A transaction is one atomic action submitted by an EOA. Inside one transaction, contracts can call other contracts many times — these inner calls are internal transactions.

The paper defines a user invocation as one internal call from the attacker-controlled contracts into the outside world. One attack transaction typically contains many user invocations (borrow, swap, deposit, …), and DeFiScope analyzes each user invocation separately — it is the unit everything below works on.

2.2 Tokens and liquidity pools

A token (WETH, USDT, …) is a balance that contracts track. A liquidity pool is a smart contract holding reserves of two (or more) tokens that anyone can trade against — a robot money-changer with a shared pot. You put token X in; it gives token Y out. There is no human market-maker; a formula decides the exchange rate.

2.3 Price models — prices are formulas over balances §II-A

This is the heart of everything. In DeFi, a token's price inside a protocol is not looked up from a market feed — it is computed by a formula whose inputs are token balances. The paper calls such a formula a price model. Three families:

(a) CPMM — Constant Product Market Maker (Uniswap and most DEXes)

The pool holds x of token X and y of token Y and enforces x · y = k (a constant). The price of X, measured in Y, is simply P(X) = y / x. A worked example with real numbers:

Worked example — one swap moves the price
Pool holds 10 ETH and 20,000 USDT → k = 200,000, price of ETH = 20,000 / 10 = 2,000 USDT.
A trader sends in 10,000 USDT. The pool must keep x·y = 200,000, so ETH balance becomes 200,000 / 30,000 = 6.67 ETH — the trader receives 3.33 ETH.
New price of ETH = 30,000 / 6.67 = 4,500 USDT.

Now look only at the balance changes: USDT in pool ↑ +10,000, ETH in pool ↓ −3.33. From those two arrows alone, the formula forces: price of ETH ↑, price of USDT (in ETH) ↓. No market data needed — direction follows from the formula plus the balance deltas. Remember this; it becomes the labeling trick in §5.

(b) Stableswap invariant (Curve)

A more complex formula used for tokens that should trade near 1:1 (e.g. two dollar-stablecoins). Its curve is nearly flat in the middle (price barely moves for normal trades) and behaves like CPMM at the extremes. You don't need its equation — just know it is a second standard, well-known formula.

(c) Custom price models — where the trouble starts

Any protocol can invent its own pricing formula. The paper's motivating case is UwULend (a lending protocol, attacked in June 2024 for $19M): it priced the token sUSDe as the median of 10 numbers — the instantaneous price of USDe in 5 different pools, plus the EMA (exponential moving average — a slow, smoothed value that stays constant within a block) price from the same 5 pools.

2.4 What a price-manipulation attack is

The attacker (usually funded by a flash loan — a huge loan that must be repaid within the same transaction, so it needs no collateral) makes a large trade not to trade, but to push a formula's output. While the price is distorted they interact with a victim protocol that trusts that formula (borrow too much, redeem too much, claim too much), then push the price back and leave with profit. All within one transaction, so nobody can react in between.

2.5 The "exchange-rate" approach used by earlier tools §II-B

Existing detectors (DeFiRanger, DeFort) define price as an exchange rate between two tokens (tokens out ÷ tokens in), track its history, and alarm when it exits a "normal fluctuation range." That presumes you can compute the rate and know what "normal" is — true for CPMM-style pools, false for custom formulas like a median-of-10.

2.6 High-level DeFi operations §V-B

Raw transaction data is just a stream of token transfers. Humans think in verbs. From a study of the top-30 DeFi apps, the paper defines six operations to recover from raw transfers:

3. The paper's DeFi findings — what they discovered & claim

Now the paper's own contributions on the DeFi side. Six findings; each later plays a specific role in the system.

FindingWhat they claimEvidenceRole in the system
F1. Custom models are common §I 44.2% of the 95 real price-manipulation attacks (2021–2024, $381M losses) target non-standard price models. Their survey of 95 attacks assembled from DeFort's list (54), DeFiHackLabs (55) and an industry partner (31), deduplicated. Motivation: any detector that only understands CPMM misses almost half the problem.
F2. Exchange-rate detection fails on them §II-B Rate + "normal range" methods are blind to subtle custom-model manipulation. UwULend: the manipulated sUSDe price moved only −4.2% / +4.43% — inside DeFort's learned bounds; DeFort misses it. Motivation for a different signal than exchange rates.
F3. Direction suffices, and is derivable §II-B You don't need the price's magnitude. The direction (up/down) per token per user invocation, combined with what operation happened, is enough — and direction follows from (price formula, balance changes) alone. The CPMM worked example above generalizes: every price model is a function of balances, so balance deltas determine the direction of its output. The core insight. Defines exactly what the LLM must do (and nothing more).
F4. Transfer Graph beats CFT §V-A Modeling each user invocation's transfers as a time-indexed directed graph (TG) recovers DeFi operations much better than DeFiRanger's Cash-Flow-Tree. On 1,000 real Ethereum transactions: true-positive rate 0.912 vs 0.559, precision 0.984 vs 0.991; +89.3% on complex multi-hop swaps. Supplies reliable "verbs" (Swap/Deposit/…) that the attack patterns are written over.
F5. Attacks follow 8 patterns §VI, Table I Across 4 protocol types, price-manipulation attacks reduce to eight 3-step templates (below). In-depth analysis of 4 representative attacks (ElephantMoney, Cream Finance, ATK, Harvest; $163.3M combined), then validated on all 95. The deterministic "judge": match any pattern → verdict True.
F6. LLMs can do F3 — but only when fine-tuned §VII-B Off-the-shelf LLMs are mediocre at code→direction reasoning; a small fine-tune fixes it. 4. AI from zero — the five ideas you need

4.1 What an LLM actually is AI

A large language model is a next-word predictor — autocomplete, scaled up enormously. It is a mathematical function with billions of adjustable numbers (parameters, or "weights"). During pre-training it read a huge amount of text and its weights were tuned so it predicts the next word well. Predicting the next word, repeatedly, is how it "answers": your text (the prompt) goes in; it generates a continuation (the response) one token at a time. A side effect of learning to continue text this well is real competence at reading code and doing step-by-step reasoning — which is exactly the competence F3 needs. Phi-3-medium is such a model, released openly by Microsoft, with 14 billion parameters.

4.2 Why the task is "score 4 statements," not "is this an attack?" §IV-A priceChangeInference.py

LLMs are unreliable judges of open-ended questions, and their answers are hard to plug into rules. So the authors made the LLM's job as narrow and machine-readable as possible. For each user invocation, it must output integer scores 1–10 for four fixed statements:

1) The price of <input token>  ... increases after change   → score?
2) The price of <input token>  ... decreases after change   → score?
3) The price of <output token> ... increases after change   → score?
4) The price of <output token> ... decreases after change   → score?

Why scores instead of yes/no? Confidence for free. For each token you compare the "increases" score against the "decreases" score: higher wins (→ INCREASE or DECREASE); a tie means the model can't tell, and the result is discarded as UNCERTAIN rather than guessed. That comparison is 5 lines of Python (utils/priceChangeInference.py, generate_finally_prediction) — no threshold to tune.

4.3 What fine-tuning is AI

Fine-tuning = continuing training on a small, focused set of example (prompt → ideal response) pairs, nudging the weights so the model imitates those examples. The base model's general knowledge stays; what changes is behavior on your task: format, procedure, domain habits. Analogy: a broadly-educated new employee (base model) given a short bootcamp of worked examples (fine-tune) — same brain, new work habits. "SFT" (supervised fine-tuning) just means: the examples come with the correct answers written out.

4.4 What LoRA is — and what r=4, alpha=8, qkv_proj/o_proj mean AI fine-tuning.py

Fully fine-tuning all 14B weights is expensive and clumsy. LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation) is the standard shortcut: freeze the whole base model and, next to a few chosen weight matrices, add a tiny trainable "correction" — two small matrices A and B whose product gets added to the frozen matrix:

Now decode every LoRA parameter the authors chose (from fine-tuning/fine-tuning.py, confirmed byte-identical in the released adapter's adapter_config.json on HuggingFace):

ParameterValuePlain-English meaningWhy this value
r (rank)4 Width of the correction channel; how "expressive" the patch is. Bigger r = more trainable numbers. The task is narrow (a scoring habit + a reasoning procedure), so a very thin patch suffices; small r also resists over-fitting 800 examples.
lora_alpha8 A scaling knob: the patch is multiplied by α/r (here 8/4 = 2) before being added. Controls how loudly the patch speaks over the frozen base. α = 2r is a common convention; a moderate gain.
target_modulesqkv_proj, o_proj WHICH matrices get a patch. These two are the entry gate and exit gate of every attention block — the machinery a transformer uses to decide which earlier words to look at (see figure below). Patching attention shapes what the model attends to and how it routes reasoning — appropriate for teaching a reading-and-reasoning procedure; it's also the classic minimal LoRA target set.
lora_dropout0.1 During training, randomly silence 10% of the patch's connections each step — a standard trick that prevents memorizing the training set. Common default for small datasets.
task_typeCAUSAL_LM "Causal language model" = the plain next-token-prediction setup of §4.1. That's what Phi-3 is.
Do the arithmetic — the 7.4 MB file IS the fine-tune
Per layer, the patches contain r×(in+out) numbers: qkv_proj → 4×(5120+7680) = 51,200; o_proj → 4×(5120+5120) = 40,960. Times 40 layers → 3,686,400 trainable parameters ≈ 0.026% of the 14-billion base. Stored at 2 bytes each → 7.37 MB — exactly the size of adapter_model.safetensors in the RocketRaccoonnn/…_lora_v2 HuggingFace repo. That small file is the entire result of the authors' training run; "loading the fine-tuned model" = loading the 28 GB base + snapping on this 7.4 MB patch.

5. The bridge: turning DeFi knowledge into a fine-tune, step by step

This is the part you asked about most. Seven steps take the findings of §3 into the 7.4 MB adapter. Watch how each step consumes a specific piece of domain knowledge.

Define the exact question the model must answer §IV-A, Fig. 2

Domain knowledge used: F3 (direction suffices). The task is frozen as: given (a) the pricing code and (b) a list of balance changes, score the four increase/decrease statements 1–10. Nothing about attacks, profits, or verdicts — those never enter the LLM's world. Narrowing the task this hard is what makes a small fine-tune feasible at all: the model must learn one skill, not "DeFi security."

Get labeled data for free — the CPMM equation is the answer key §IV-A

Domain knowledge used: the CPMM formula itself (§2.3a). Training needs examples with correct answers. Manually labeling thousands of "did the price go up?" cases would be slow and error-prone. The authors' cleverest move: for a CPMM pool, the correct direction is computable — P(X)=y/x, so any balance change pair mechanically implies the direction. The domain equation becomes the labeling function. Zero human labeling for the raw facts; humans only review the wording later (step 5).

Analogy
It's a crash-test lab: instead of collecting real accidents (rare, messy, unlabeled), crash cars in a lab where physics tells you the exact outcome every time. Physics here = x·y=k.

Manufacture the data — Foundry simulation §IV-A

Domain knowledge used: how real swaps work (§2.2–2.3). They used Foundry (a standard Ethereum developer toolkit) to fork the real blockchain at block height 17,949,214 (August 2023) — a private sandbox copy where you can execute anything without spending money — and pointed its fuzzer (an automatic random-input generator) at the real Uniswap V2 BTC20/WETH pool:

Why these choices?

  • Why BTC20 and a forked simulation? To avoid data leakage — the situation where a model looks smart only because it memorized the test material during pre-training. BTC20 was a fresh token, and simulated swaps never existed on-chain, so no LLM can have memorized them.
  • Why 100–1000 ETH per swap? Big enough to move a pool visibly — the size range of real manipulation trades, not retail noise.
  • Why only CPMM data? Only CPMM gives free labels (step 2). Whether skills learned on CPMM transfer to custom formulas is exactly what the evaluation must check — it does, surprisingly well (§6.3).

Wrap each pair in a chain-of-thought prompt §IV-A, Fig. 2 dataset/training_set.csv

Domain knowledge used: F3's two-stage structure. Each training prompt has two instructions that force a procedure, not just an answer — this is "chain-of-thought" (CoT): make the model show its working like a math student. The real thing, from row 1 of dataset/training_set.csv (the actual file in this repo, 800 rows):

Note where the pieces of the puzzle went: the pricing code feeds Instruction 1; the Foundry balance deltas fill the <change> block; F3's four-direction question becomes the four statements; and the rigid <answer> format exists so five lines of Python can parse the reply.

Write the ideal responses — model drafts, humans correct §IV-A

Fine-tuning needs the target response text, not just the right label. Writing 1,000 step-by-step explanations by hand is expensive, so: (1) ask an LLM to draft a full response for each prompt (analysis of the code, then the four scores); (2) a human checks each draft against the free CPMM label; (3) if the direction is wrong, fix it — mechanically, by swapping the scores of the opposite statements. The result (again real, from row 1 of the shipped CSV): reasoning first, then

<answer>
1) ... increases after change": 2
2) ... decreases after change": 8      ← input token: DOWN (confident)
3) ... increases after change": 8      ← output token: UP (confident)
4) ... decreases after change": 2
</answer>

So each training example teaches simultaneously: the procedure (extract formula → reason over deltas), the direction mapping (reserve up ⇒ that token's price down), and the output format.

Run it — what "training" mechanically does fine-tuning.py

For each batch: feed prompt + ideal response through the model; at every token position the model predicts the next token; compute the loss only over the response tokens (the prompt positions are masked out with the label -100, the "don't grade this part" marker — you can see it in fine-tuning.py's preprocess_function); nudge only the LoRA patches downhill; repeat for 10 epochs.

The output of the run = the trained A/B patches = adapter_model.safetensors (7.4 MB) + adapter_config.json. The authors uploaded exactly these two files to HuggingFace as RocketRaccoonnn/Phi-3-medium-128k-instruct_LoRA_CASUAL_LM_lora_v2 — the artifact this repo downloads and runs. (Paper reference [70] points to this exact URL.)

Honest footnote for re-runners: the shipped script formats training text as "Task: {input}\nResponse:\n{response}", and its DeepSpeed config file (lora_ds_config.json) is missing from the repo — its contents are recoverable from model_comparison_result.md. Neither matters for using the released adapter.

6. How they proved the fine-tuning works — the evidence ladder

"It seems better" is not proof. The paper climbs five rungs, each answering a different skeptical question.

6.1 Rung 1 — Does the sensor itself get the direction right? §IV-B

On the held-out test set (100 price-change cases the model never trained on): Type-I prompts: 99% correct direction. Type-II prompts: 97%. (Type-II is the fallback when a pool's source code isn't published: the prompt simply asserts "this pool follows CPMM" instead of pasting code — valid because two-token pools overwhelmingly are CPMMs.) So the sensor is accurate in isolation before it's ever plugged into the detector.

6.2 Rung 2 — Does fine-tuning improve the whole detector? (ablation) §VII-B, Fig. 6

An ablation = remove one component, measure the drop. Here: run the identical pipeline with the base model vs the fine-tuned one. First, a structural fact: of the 95 attacks, only 78 ever reach the LLM (8 lack verified source / fail compilation; 9 are cross-transaction or non-standard-token cases). Results over those 78 (GPT models; the paper gives no per-category split for Phi-3):

6.3 Rung 3 — The transfer surprise: trained on CPMM only, wins on custom models

The training data was 100% CPMM (step 3's simulator). If fine-tuning had merely memorized CPMM facts, custom-model attacks would not improve. They improved dramatically: custom-model detection went 60% → 93.3% (GPT-3.5) and 86.7% → 96.7% (GPT-4o). The authors' explanation, from reading the models' responses: the biggest change is that the fine-tuned model strictly follows the two-step procedure — extract the price formula first, then reason from it plus the deltas — whereas the base model jumps to scores without doing the analysis. In other words, the fine-tune taught a reasoning ritual, not a formula — and rituals transfer to formulas never seen in training. This is the paper's deepest empirical claim.

6.4 Rung 4 — Does the method survive changing the model? Phi-3 — our baseline §VIII, Table VI

To show the recipe isn't an artifact of OpenAI's fine-tuning service, they re-applied it (as plain LoRA SFT, steps 5–7 above) to the open Phi-3-medium — 4× H800 GPUs, all 1,000 samples:

6.5 Rung 5 — Does the whole system beat the state of the art? §VII-A/C

With the (private) GPT-3.5 fine-tune installed: recall 80% on D1 vs DeFort 52.6%, DeFiRanger 51.6%, DeFiTainter 35.8%. On 968 real suspicious transactions (D2): 153 flagged, 147 manually confirmed → precision 96%, including 81 previously undocumented attacks. On 96,800 benign transactions (D3): zero false alarms, ~2.5 s/transaction. These headline numbers establish the method; they are not reproducible by us (private model, unreleased D2/D3 labels) — which is precisely why the released Phi-3 with its honest 0.66 is the baseline.

Read the ladder as a whole
Rung 1 isolates the sensor; rung 2 shows the sensor's quality is what moves the whole detector; rung 3 shows the learning is procedural (so it generalizes); rung 4 shows it's model-agnostic (and gives us a public artifact); rung 5 shows the assembled system beats prior tools. Each rung would be unconvincing alone; together they are the paper's argument that the fine-tuning — not luck, leakage, or the rules — is what works.

7. How the fine-tuned Phi-3 produces 0.66 in this repo

7.1 The pipeline the sensor sits in Fig. 1 main.py

7.2 What Phi-3 sees and answers priceChangeInference.py

Per user invocation the repo generates exactly the prompt style of §5 step 4 — Type-I when the contract's verified source was fetched (via Etherscan), else Type-II ("0x… is the address of a liquidity pool. The price model of the pool aligns with the Constant Product Market Maker (CPMM)…"). The reply's four scores are parsed by regex, then reduced pairwise:

# utils/priceChangeInference.py — the entire "judgment" on the model's output
if   increase_score > decrease_score:  tendency = INCREASE
elif increase_score < decrease_score:  tendency = DECREASE
else:                                  tendency = UNCERTAIN   # tie → discard
The silent-benign trap (worth knowing before trusting any batch run)
If the model call fails (bad key, OOM, network), the code catches the error and sets all scores to 0 — a tie — so every direction becomes UNCERTAIN, no pattern can fire, and the verdict is a quiet "False." A totally broken setup thus looks like "no attacks found." Always confirm the model is genuinely answering (e.g. run the known-attack AES transaction first) before believing recall numbers.

8. Common confusions, answered

"Is the RocketRaccoonnn model the 0.52 one, which becomes 0.66 after we fine-tune it with the repo's script?"
No — the RocketRaccoonnn adapter IS the finished fine-tune = the 0.66 row. 0.52 is plain Phi-3 with no adapter. A LoRA adapter is the output of training, not an input to it; downloading it means the training is already done. You never run fine-tuning.py to reach 0.66 — you'd only run it to train a new adapter of your own.
"What does the [70] in 'Phi-3 w/ fine-tuning [70]' mean?"
It's a bibliography citation, not a version number. Reference [70] in the paper is literally the HuggingFace URL of the RocketRaccoonnn adapter — the paper citing its own released artifact.
"Then what is fine-tuning/fine-tuning.py for?"
It's the recipe that produced the adapter (and the proof of how it was made — its LoRA numbers match the released adapter exactly). Useful if you want to re-train with different data/params to try to beat 0.66; irrelevant for reproducing 0.66.
"Why can't we get the paper's 0.80 / 96% / zero-false-alarm numbers?"
0.80 needs the authors' fine-tuned GPT-3.5, which lives only inside their OpenAI account. 96% and zero-FP need the D2/D3 ground-truth labels, which were never released. The public, reproducible claim is exactly one number: Phi-3-FT recall ≈ 0.66 on D1.
"Is 0.66 the model's accuracy?"
No — it's the whole system's recall with Phi-3 as the sensor. The model's own measured skill is the 99%/97% direction accuracy (rung 1). 0.66 mixes model quality with harness limits (the 0.82 ceiling) and the rules' coverage. Keep the two numbers separate when writing about the baseline.
